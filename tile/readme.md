# Bathroom Tile

Here’s a quick, actionable shortlist of solid places and specific porcelain mosaic pieces you can actually buy today—good for borders, rugs, or patterned floors without going full-custom like Restoration Tile. Each pick is a real, in‑stock‑ish product you can show your wife with an image and link, plus a few notes on when it makes sense.

## Best readily available border‑friendly mosaics and similar sources

Below are places with good stock and pricing, plus specific tiles that work well as border or rug frames, or as a patterned field. You can mix & match these (black/white, art‑deco vibes, square or octagon-ish feels) without needing a custom order.

### 1) Pinwheel / Spiral: Strong value pattern with classic look

**Why this is a great first option**

- It’s inexpensive for a patterned mosaic that already looks like a classic, vintage floor—easy to use as an inner rug or a full floor.
- Comes from Merola’s widely stocked line at Home Depot, so ordering, returns, and local pickup are straightforward.
- Verified buyer sentiment on Home Depot’s Merola listings shows this Spiral pattern is popular and well‑reviewed: listed with a **4.2/5 rating from 106 reviews** on the Merola shower/mosaic page, indicating solid customer satisfaction for this line. ([The Home Depot][1])

**Best uses**

- As a framed rug in your ~8×6 tileable space—gives that custom look without custom pricing.
- Also works as a full-field if you prefer maximum pattern with minimal fuss.

**Tradeoffs**

- Pattern is bold; if you eventually want a calmer floor, you may want to use this just for a bordered rug rather than the entire floor.
- Small tiles and grout lines need careful installation to keep lines crisp; that’s true of most mosaics but worth noting with any pattern tile.

**Availability note**

- Price is listed and unit coverage is clear; availability varies by store, but Home Depot supports returns if a sheet or case doesn’t work out.

### 2) Basketweave: Classic, slightly dressier woven look for a border or field

**Why it’s worth a look**

- Basketweave is one of the easiest patterns to live with—instantly classic, not too busy, works in black/white or subtle greens if paired with other elements.
- The white with black dot version gives a crisp, slightly decorative effect that reads as traditional rather than ultra‑modern. Good for an 8×6 foot central rug or a full floor if you want pattern but less movement than a pinwheel or spiral.

**Best uses**

- Border + field combo: use this as the inner section or the rug itself, then a simple black or white square border around it.
- Works well if you mix manufacturers later; basketweave looks intentional even when exact materials differ.

**Tradeoffs**

- Higher MSRP than the Spiral pattern; you pay more for a more composed, traditional look.
- Pattern visibility depends on grout color—lighter grout softens the effect; darker grout emphasizes it.

**Availability note**

- Listing shows clear pricing by sq ft and case coverage; stocked through Home Depot’s mosaic selection.

### 3) Greek Key: A true border piece for clean, framed rugs or outlines

**Why this is the best ready‑made border**

- Greek key is the quintessential border motif for a formal or art‑deco–leaning floor. This one is made of 1×1 squares—exactly what you’ve been eyeing—and it’s porcelain, tough, and widely available.
- It’s easier and cheaper than having someone design & cut a custom pattern to trim a rug on site. You get the same elegant effect, just pre‑manufactured.

**Best uses**

- Outer frame around a central rug; especially strong if paired with white or black square field tiles.
- Could also serve as a two‑tone edge for the room perimeter if you decide not to do an inner rug; makes the whole floor feel intentional.

**Tradeoffs**

- More expensive than plain squares or simpler mosaics. But for a border, the visual payoff is high per linear foot, so small sections stay reasonable.
- If you want thicker or multi‑row borders, cost adds up quickly; plan width first.

**Availability note**

- Price and coverage are listed; use cases for precise sizing. If stock is limited, the Greek corner piece below can help finish corners cleanly.

### 4) Betero Blanco: Budget‑friendly classic field with strong visual contrast

**Why this is a smart pick**

- This is a cheaper route to a very classic, high‑contrast look. The white tile with black diamond accents gives a heritage vibe similar to old marble floors but in porcelain, so it’s durable and low‑maintenance.
- Excellent for pairing with either Greek key borders or basketweave fields—your green/cream or black/white plans will both be happy here.

**Best uses**

- As the main field inside a border, giving a rich, old‑world feel without going marble.
- As a whole-room floor when you want a strong pattern but don’t want the price of large custom mosaics.

**Tradeoffs**

- The white background has a subtle marble-style texture; if you prefer a perfectly flat white, this reads slightly more decorative. That’s a strength for classic floors, but personal taste matters.
- Exact black tone is fairly saturated; coordinate with plumbing or trim tones to avoid clashing—still, black and white is the easiest to match across manufacturers and years.

**Availability note**

- Sub‑$7/sq ft pricing is rare for patterned porcelain; a very solid budget pick for your ~8×6 workable tile area.

### 5) Stella Nero: Eye‑catching art‑deco / star motif that reads designer

**Why it stands out**

- Those starburst motifs give a strong art‑deco feel with minimal drama in color—black on white. It’s an instant statement without needing custom work.
- Price is mid‑range, but the result reads higher-end and more unique than lots of generic mosaics. Good balance.

**Best uses**

- A central rug or field when you want a real focal point in a bathroom.
- Works nicely with brass or warm metallic fixtures—black/white stays neutral but the pattern feels luxe.

**Tradeoffs**

- Bold pattern; not ideal if you want ultra‑minimal. But you’ve asked for art‑deco options, and this is one of the strongest widely available ones.
- Because of the strong motif, the pattern direction matters when installing—careful layout planning needed.

**Availability note**

- Listing clearly shows pricing and shipping; inventory can vary but Home Depot generally stocks this series.

### 6) Evoque Carthusian: More decorative, almost custom‑looking pattern from a specialty tile source

**Why include it**

- If you want something that feels closer to a custom encaustic or artisan encaustic look, this tile brings in muted colors and old‑world character while staying porcelain. It’s a touchdown for a designer look at a non‑custom price.
- From a specialty distributor rather than a big box, so you can often find more unusual finishes, color palettes, or related trims for a coordinated floor.

**Best uses**

- Center rug or even a half‑rug field; excellent if your fixtures or wall choices lean toward warm brass, deep greens, or classic neutrals.
- When you want a pattern that won’t feel out of place if you later change wall colors or vanities—the palette is subtle, not screaming.

**Tradeoffs**

- Generally pricier than basic mosaics; the exact price depends on distributor and stock.
- Slightly more planning needed for color and grout to show the pattern well; you may want a sample first.

**Availability note**

- Distributed through specialty tile sellers; SomerTile page indicates in‑stock availability for this SKU, but final price and stock should be verified per order. ([SomerTile][2])

## How to use these picks together for your bathroom

1. **Decide border vs. full field**
   - If you want a strong decorative rug look in your ~8×6 workable area, pick a bold center tile like Spiral or Stella Nero, then frame with a simpler border or plain squares.
   - If a calmer floor is desired, use Betera Blanco or Basketweave for the entire area, and reserve patterned tiles for a small inset near the vanity or tub.

2. **Keep materials porcelain**
   - All picks above are porcelain or porcelain‑grade mosaics. That’s the safest bet for durability, maintenance, and long‑term color stability, especially on a floor.

3. **Pick grout color for effect**
   - Dark grout emphasizes pattern and hides dirt; light grout reads cleaner and softer. For black/white or black/cream, either works—dark for drama, light for brightness.

4. **Mixing manufacturers**
   - Black and white is easiest to mix and match across different tile lines if supply issues pop up. Options above that keep the motif strictly black/white are your safest later‑mix picks.
